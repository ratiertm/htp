# Free Energy Principle and Active Inference

The free energy principle proposes that biological systems maintain their structural
integrity by minimizing a free energy functional that bounds surprise on sensory
input. Free energy provides a tractable proxy for the marginal likelihood that
appears in Bayesian inference. By minimizing variational free energy with respect to
internal beliefs, the brain implements approximate Bayesian inference over hidden
causes of sensory data.

Active inference extends free energy minimization to action selection. Agents
sample actions that minimize expected free energy, balancing epistemic exploration
that reduces uncertainty about world states with pragmatic exploitation that
fulfills prior preferences. This framework unifies perception, learning, and action
under a single optimization principle. Hierarchical predictive coding emerges as one
implementation of the free energy principle in the cortex, with prediction errors
weighted by precision parameters that gate the influence of sensory evidence on
belief updating. The framework has been applied to perception, motor control,
decision making, and psychiatric conditions.
