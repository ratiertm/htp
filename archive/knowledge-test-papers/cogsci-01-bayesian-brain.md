# Bayesian Brain Hypothesis and Predictive Coding

The Bayesian brain hypothesis proposes that the cortex implements probabilistic
inference over hierarchical generative models of sensory causes. Perception arises
not from passive feature detection but from active inference that combines prior
expectations with incoming sensory evidence according to Bayes rule. Belief updating
proceeds through prediction error signals that propagate up the cortical hierarchy
while predictions flow downward.

Predictive coding instantiates this hypothesis as a specific neural implementation.
Each level of the cortical hierarchy maintains expectations about activity in the
level below, computes the prediction error, and transmits residuals upward.
Hierarchical Bayesian inference enables the brain to represent multiple
spatiotemporal scales and causal relationships within a unified probability space.
Empirical support comes from neuroimaging studies of expectation effects, predictive
suppression in early sensory areas, and prediction error signals in association
cortex. Critical perspectives note challenges of computational tractability and
biological plausibility of exact Bayesian inference.
