# Canonical Cortical Microcircuits and Laminar Computation

The mammalian neocortex exhibits a stereotyped six layer architecture replicated
across functional regions. This canonical microcircuit motif suggests common
computational primitives implemented by laminar specific cell types and connectivity
patterns. Layer 4 receives thalamic input, layers 2 and 3 perform local
computation, layer 5 outputs to subcortical structures, and layer 6 projects back
to thalamus.

Excitatory pyramidal neurons in different layers exhibit distinct morphology, gene
expression, and intrinsic firing properties. Inhibitory interneurons including
parvalbumin, somatostatin, and VIP cells provide diverse temporal and spatial
patterns of inhibition. Recurrent connections within and between layers implement
attractor dynamics, gain control, and competition. Recent theoretical proposals
interpret cortical microcircuits as implementing predictive coding, with deep
layers carrying predictions and superficial layers transmitting prediction errors.
Understanding cortical computation requires linking detailed circuit anatomy to
algorithmic descriptions of information processing.
